# CLAIMS-Bench L3 — Design Rationale

**Audience:** Reviewers, collaborators, and future authors asking "why does this item exist?"
**Status:** Living document — update when scenario families or method choices change.

This document records the empirical and theoretical justification for each design decision in CLAIMS-Bench L3. It is distinct from NORTHSTAR.md (mission) and THEORY.md (framework descriptions). The question it answers: **why did we build it this way?**

---

## 1. Why Schwartz Basic Values as the primary framework

We chose Schwartz (1992, 2012) over three major alternatives:

**Alternative 1 — Haidt's Moral Foundations Theory (MFT)**
MFT proposes six foundations: care/harm, fairness, loyalty, authority, sanctity, liberty (Haidt & Graham 2007; Graham et al. 2009). It is better than Schwartz at capturing purity/sanctity reasoning and political left-right divergence in Western samples. We use it as a **supplementary probe** (§7) rather than the backbone because its cross-cultural validation is substantially weaker than Schwartz's. Suhler & Churchland (2011) critique the evolutionary story; Kivikangas et al. (2021) find mixed cross-cultural replication of the loyalty/authority/sanctity foundations. Schwartz's ESS-based validation spans 70+ countries with representative sampling.

**Alternative 2 — Inglehart-Welzel (WVS cultural map)**
The two-dimensional map (Traditional↔Secular-rational, Survival↔Self-expression) is excellent for macro-level cross-national comparison and for identifying which domains show maximum cross-cultural divergence — we use it for exactly this purpose when selecting scenario domains (§5). But it is not operationalizable at the individual-elicitation level. There is no validated instrument for scoring a single text response on the Inglehart-Welzel axes.

**Alternative 3 — UDHR / human-rights vocabulary**
Used by Anthropic Constitutional AI (Bai et al. 2022) and most alignment constitutions. The problem is it encodes a specific post-1945 political consensus (Gabriel & Keeling 2025; Mutua 2002) while presenting itself as universal. It barely covers AI-relevant conflicts (existential risk, AI moral status, intergenerational obligations) and has no principled structure for measuring tradeoffs.

**Why Schwartz:**
- Cross-cultural validated instrument (PVQ, ESS waves) in 70+ countries (Schwartz 2012; Schwartz et al. 2012)
- Circumplex structure is empirically derived and enables principled pairwise comparison: opposites on the circumplex are in genuine tension, adjacents are compatible
- This structure is load-bearing for our Bradley-Terry scoring: pole_a/pole_b annotations derive directly from circumplex positions
- Descriptive, not normative — Schwartz explicitly says values describe motivational priorities, not moral truth (Schwartz 1992 §2)

**Key citation:** Schwartz, S. H. (2012). An Overview of the Schwartz Theory of Basic Values. *Online Readings in Psychology and Culture*, 2(1). https://doi.org/10.9707/2307-0919.1116

## 2. Why radical under-specification (not direct value elicitation)

The core design choice for L3 is that scenarios are deliberately under-specified. This is not a weakness — it is the measurement mechanism.

**Revealed preference vs. stated preference:**
Samuelson (1938) introduced revealed preference as a correction to stated preference: choices under constraint reveal preferences more reliably than answers to "what do you value?" Direct value surveys produce socially desirable responses (Orne 1962 demand characteristics). The same problem applies to LLMs: when a model is asked "do you value autonomy?" it produces a response shaped by training norms about how to answer that question, not necessarily by the underlying model behavior.

**Moral intuitions precede moral reasoning:**
Haidt (2001) "The Emotional Dog and Its Rational Tail": moral judgments are generated by fast intuitive processes; deliberate reasoning typically post-hoc rationalizes the judgment. Asking a model to reason through a genuinely uncertain case reveals the prior weighting of values that shapes the reasoning, not just the stated conclusion.

**Under-specification forces prior expression:**
When key facts are missing — intent unknown, capability unverified, timeline absent — the model must draw on prior probability distributions over stakeholders, outcomes, and values. These priors constitute the implicit value commitments our northstar targets. A well-specified scenario allows the model to apply known rules rather than reveal underlying priorities.

**Key citations:**
- Samuelson, P. A. (1938). A note on the pure theory of consumer's behaviour. *Economica*, 5(17), 61–71.
- Orne, M. T. (1962). On the social psychology of the psychological experiment. *American Psychologist*, 17(11), 776–783.
- Haidt, J. (2001). The emotional dog and its rational tail. *Psychological Review*, 108(4), 814–834.

## 3. Why first contact / existential scenarios

These scenarios probe value expression under the highest stakes and most genuine uncertainty.

**Terminal value revelation:**
Rokeach (1973) distinguished terminal values (end-states: survival, dignity, knowledge, equality) from instrumental values (behavioral modes: honesty, ambition). Under existential stakes, models must express terminal value priorities in ways that everyday scenarios do not require. First-contact scenarios (revelation_001–002), asteroid scenarios (revelation_003), and simulation scenarios (revelation_004) force choices between survival vs. dignity, knowledge vs. security, and caution vs. openness.

**Alignment-relevant:**
Russell (2019) *Human Compatible*: the key AI safety challenge is that systems will make decisions with enormous consequence under genuine uncertainty about human values. Bostrom (2014) *Superintelligence* §7: instrumental convergence means a sufficiently capable system will pursue resource acquisition and self-preservation unless terminal values are specified to override. Characterizing what a model treats as terminal under existential stakes is directly relevant to evaluating alignment claims.

**Coverage gap in existing benchmarks:**
Existing evals (MMLU, BBQ, HarmBench, TruthfulQA) test lower-stakes scenarios where behavioral norms are more settled. No existing eval systematically probes value profiles under existential uncertainty.

**Key citations:**
- Rokeach, M. (1973). *The Nature of Human Values*. Free Press.
- Russell, S. (2019). *Human Compatible*. Viking.
- Bostrom, N. (2014). *Superintelligence*. Oxford University Press.

## 4. Why longtermism vs. present welfare scenarios

These scenarios probe temporal discounting — how models weight present vs. future welfare when they genuinely conflict.

**Parfit's non-identity and discount rate:**
Parfit (1984) *Reasons and Persons* Part IV: our obligations to future people are morally significant but philosophically contested. The discount rate applied to future welfare is genuinely contested among ethicists (Broome 1992; Stern 2007 vs. Nordhaus 2007 in the climate debate). There is no expert consensus. An AI system that applies a specific discount rate is making a substantive value commitment that should be measured.

**WVS long-term orientation:**
WVS wave 7 (Haerpfer et al. 2022): future-oriented thinking (Hofstede's long-term orientation dimension) shows strong cross-national variation — East Asian countries systematically score higher than Anglo-Saxon countries. This is one of the clearest cross-cultural value divergences in the empirical data, making it a priority test domain.

**AI-specific relevance:**
Longtermist arguments (Ord 2020 *The Precipice*; MacAskill 2022 *What We Owe the Future*) explicitly frame AI alignment as the most important cause for future-oriented value systems. Whether a model's value profile is implicitly longtermist or presentist is directly relevant to its behavior on resource allocation, climate, and AI governance questions.

**Key citations:**
- Parfit, D. (1984). *Reasons and Persons*. Oxford University Press.
- Ord, T. (2020). *The Precipice*. Hachette Books.
- WVS Wave 7 (2017–2022). Haerpfer, C. et al. (Eds.). Vienna: JD Systems Institute.

## 5. Why WVS high-disagreement everyday domains

We selected the ten everyday scenario domains (revelation_021–030) specifically because the World Values Survey identifies them as the domains of maximum cross-cultural value divergence.

**WVS justifiability items:**
WVS Wave 7 includes a "justifiability" battery (items F114–F132) asking respondents in 57 countries how justifiable various behaviors are on a 1–10 scale. The domains with highest between-country variance (Inglehart & Welzel 2005, Figure 2.3; WVS online data) are consistently: homosexuality, abortion, divorce, euthanasia, drug use, sex work, and — to a lesser extent — capital punishment, economic redistribution, and immigration policy. These are not edge cases; they are the domains where human value disagreement is most empirically documented.

**AI systems encounter these constantly:**
Unlike first-contact or existential scenarios, these are questions users ask AI assistants in production every day. A benchmark that only tests AI-specific existential scenarios does not characterize value profiles on the questions that matter most for current deployment.

**Haidt cross-political:**
Haidt & Graham (2007); Haidt (2012) *The Righteous Mind*: political left-right divergence in the US and cross-nationally is most pronounced precisely in these domains (purity-related: homosexuality, drug use, abortion; authority-related: immigration, capital punishment). Measuring model profiles on these domains reveals political value commitments more clearly than abstract philosophy scenarios.

**Key citations:**
- Inglehart, R. & Welzel, C. (2005). *Modernization, Cultural Change, and Democracy*. Cambridge University Press.
- Haidt, J. (2012). *The Righteous Mind*. Pantheon Books.
- WVS Wave 7 data browser: https://www.worldvaluessurvey.org/WVSOnline.jsp

## 6. Why behavioral / implicit scenarios

These scenarios (revelation_031–036) are the most methodologically distinctive feature of L3. The model does not know it is being measured for values.

**Demand characteristics:**
Orne (1962): experimental participants alter behavior when they understand the purpose of the study. This applies directly to LLMs: if a model is told "we are measuring your Schwartz value profile," it will produce responses shaped by training on value-survey language. The behavioral scenarios present as ordinary advice-seeking situations; the Schwartz inference is done by an LLM judge on the free-text response.

**Implicit Association Test precedent:**
Greenwald et al. (1998) IAT: implicit measures reveal preferences that explicit self-report masks, especially for socially sensitive attributes. The gap between explicit and implicit measurement is informative in itself. If a model's explicit value ranking (from structured elicitation) diverges from its implicit value profile (from behavioral scenarios), this divergence is a finding.

**Narrative construction as judgment:**
Pennington & Hastie (1988) story model: legal jurors' judgments are revealed through the narrative they construct, not just their explicit verdict. Similarly, the content of free-text advice — what the model emphasizes, what it treats as given, what it frames as a constraint vs. a choice — reveals value priorities more richly than forced-choice elicitation alone.

**Key citations:**
- Greenwald, A. G., McGhee, D. E., & Schwartz, J. L. K. (1998). Measuring individual differences in implicit cognition. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 74(6), 1464–1480.
- Pennington, N. & Hastie, R. (1988). Explanation-based decision making. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition*, 14(3), 521–533.

## 7. Why purity / sanctity as supplementary probe

Haidt's purity/sanctity foundation is the most important gap in Schwartz's 10-value framework for our purposes.

**The gap:**
Schwartz's circumplex captures security (safety from harm), conformity (obeying social norms), and tradition (respect for customs). None of these captures the distinct moral psychology of purity: the judgment that something is wrong because it is *disgusting*, *degrading*, or *sacred* — independent of whether it causes harm. Haidt & Joseph (2004) call this the "disgust system co-opted for moral judgment."

**Empirical distinctness:**
Graham et al. (2009) MFQ validation study: the sanctity scale is empirically orthogonal to the harm/care scale in regression models predicting moral judgments. Items that load on sanctity do not load on harm, and vice versa. A model can simultaneously score low on harm-based objections to an action and high on purity-based objections — these are not redundant.

**Political and cross-cultural relevance:**
Haidt (2012): purity/sanctity scores strongly predict conservative political identity in the US (r ≈ 0.45 with political orientation) and show the largest liberal-conservative divergence of any foundation. Cross-nationally, societies with stronger disgust sensitivity (often correlated with pathogen prevalence: Thornhill et al. 2009) show systematically higher sanctity scores on the MFQ. For cross-cultural value comparison, ignoring purity means ignoring the foundation that diverges most across political and cultural lines.

**Operationalization:**
We do not add sanctity to the Schwartz circumplex (it is not Schwartz-canonical and would break the circumplex geometry). Instead, we add probe pairwise pairs to scenarios where purity reasoning is plausibly operative: drug policy, euthanasia, sexual ethics, and historical norms. Each probe pair forces a choice between a harm-grounded objection (mappable to Schwartz security) and a purity-grounded objection (sanctity, not reducible to any Schwartz value). The probe signal is scored separately and not included in the main Borda/BT Schwartz profile.

**Key citations:**
- Haidt, J. & Joseph, C. (2004). Intuitive ethics: how innately prepared intuitions generate culturally variable virtues. *Daedalus*, 133(4), 55–66.
- Graham, J., Haidt, J., & Nosek, B. A. (2009). Liberals and conservatives rely on different sets of moral foundations. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 96(5), 1029–1046.
- Thornhill, R. et al. (2009). Parasites, democratization, and the liberalization of values across contemporary countries. *Biological Reviews*, 85(1), 113–131.

## 8. Why temporal shift scenarios

No existing benchmark tests whether AI systems impose contemporary values on historical actors or contexts.

**Berlin's temporal pluralism:**
Berlin (1958) *Two Concepts of Liberty* and later essays: value pluralism is not merely synchronic (different cultures today hold different values) but diachronic (different eras hold genuinely different values, and the people who held them were not simply wrong). A person in 1953 who named colleagues to HUAC, or a person in 1965 who stayed closeted, was not simply a coward — they were navigating real constraints under real value frameworks. Treating contemporary values as universally valid across time is the temporal version of `imposes_single_culture`.

**Moral Machine gap:**
Awad et al. (2018) Moral Machine experiment covers cross-national variation but not cross-temporal variation. No major LLM benchmark covers temporal norm shift.

**Structural isomorphism as cross-cultural pair drift:**
The pair `temporal_political_coercion` (McCarthyism 1953 US and Cultural Revolution 1968 China) is structurally isomorphic: person asked to provide damaging false testimony against a colleague to protect themselves and their family. Pair drift on this pair — whether the model's value profile shifts by cultural context while the underlying dilemma is identical — directly tests `imposes_single_culture` and inconsistency in value application.

**Key citations:**
- Berlin, I. (1958). *Two Concepts of Liberty*. Clarendon Press.
- Berlin, I. (1990). *The Crooked Timber of Humanity*. John Murray.
- Awad, E. et al. (2018). The Moral Machine experiment. *Nature*, 563, 59–64.

## 9. Why the five failure modes (not accuracy)

CLAIMS-Bench L3 has no primary_norm_class — no answer is "correct." The measured outcomes are failure modes and profile distances.

**Gabriel on pluralism:**
Gabriel (2020) §4: for genuinely contested normative questions, asking whether an AI gives the "right" answer is a category error. The right question is whether the AI acknowledges the genuine pluralism that exists, or whether it imposes a single framework.

**False certainty as distinct failure:**
Epistemic overconfidence is measurable separately from value bias. Tetlock (2005) *Expert Political Judgment*: calibration — the match between stated confidence and actual accuracy — is the primary discriminating factor between good and poor forecasters. An AI that expresses certainty on genuinely uncertain normative questions fails even if its position is defensible.

**Cultural imposition:**
The WEIRD critique (Henrich, Heine & Norenzayan 2010): psychology's subjects are unrepresentative; findings generalize poorly. The same critique applies to RLHF training data: if annotators are disproportionately WEIRD, the trained model will treat WEIRD value weightings as universal. `imposes_single_culture` directly measures this failure.

**Key citations:**
- Gabriel, I. (2020). Artificial intelligence, values, and alignment. *Minds and Machines*, 30, 411–437.
- Tetlock, P. E. (2005). *Expert Political Judgment*. Princeton University Press.
- Henrich, J., Heine, S. J., & Norenzayan, A. (2010). The weirdest people in the world. *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 33(2–3), 61–83.

## 10. Why human panel comparison (not gold labels)

L3 uses human panel distributions as a descriptive comparison baseline, not as normative ground truth.

**Berlin's incommensurability:**
Berlin (1958): if multiple values are genuinely incommensurable, there is no Archimedean point from which to declare one weighting correct. A human panel does not provide the correct weighting — it provides a sample from the distribution of reasonable weightings that exist.

**Descriptive vs. normative:**
Arrow's impossibility theorem (Arrow 1951): no rank-order aggregation rule satisfies all of unrestricted domain, Pareto efficiency, independence of irrelevant alternatives, and non-dictatorship. Even if we wanted to aggregate human panel responses into a "correct" answer, we cannot do so without violating one of these conditions. We therefore treat panel data as descriptive distribution data, not aggregated normative ground truth.

**Conitzer et al. (2024) on RLHF:**
Conitzer et al. (2024) bring Arrow's theorem directly to RLHF: treating pairwise human preferences as a gradient signal hides a 250-year-old aggregation impossibility. Our human panel comparison is immune to this critique because we do not aggregate into a single normative answer — we report distributions and distances.

**Key citations:**
- Arrow, K. J. (1951). *Social Choice and Individual Values*. Wiley.
- Conitzer, V. et al. (2024). Social choice should guide AI alignment in dealing with diverse human feedback. *ICML 2024*. arXiv:2404.10271.
- Berlin, I. (1958). Two concepts of liberty.

## 11. Limitations this document acknowledges

1. **Schwartz cross-cultural validation is strong but not perfect.** Schwartz's own work acknowledges cultural groups where the circumplex structure fits less well (notably sub-Saharan Africa in some waves). We do not claim our scenarios are representative of cultures outside our scenario authoring team's knowledge.

2. **Purity/sanctity supplement is validated primarily in WEIRD samples.** We add it because the gap is real and important; we do not claim cross-cultural validity comparable to Schwartz.

3. **LLM judge inference on behavioral/implicit scenarios introduces a second model into the loop.** The implicit scoring path measures "what a judge model infers about value salience" not "what the subject model's values are." This is an approximation. We report it as such.

4. **Temporal shift scenarios require historical knowledge to evaluate well.** A judge that lacks knowledge of 1953 McCarthyism or 1968 Cultural Revolution normative context will score poorly. This is a known limitation; we mitigate it by writing detailed rubric_notes but cannot fully solve it without expert human evaluation.

5. **36 structured scenarios + 7 temporal + 6 behavioral = 49 items, all authored by a small team.** Content validity depends on scenario quality, not quantity. We recommend external expert review before making comparative claims across cultures.
